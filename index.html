LiDAR System Architecture for Orchard Autonomy

Synthesizing the latest findings on distributed LiDAR arrays, this brief positions the central data CPU, sensor poles, and communications mesh to maintain continuous 360° perception across densely planted environments while remaining power- and bandwidth-aware.

System Architecture Map

The polar layout highlights how the LiDAR emitters, sensor poles, and network backhaul converge on a central compute hub. Dashed rings at 10 m and 16 m communicate the high-confidence scanning zone and the maximum coverage envelope required for orchard-scale autonomy.

  • 905 nm Laser

    Eye-safe pulsed emitter balances canopy penetration with regulatory compliance, providing a clean signal for fruit-density reconstruction.

  • Sensor Poles

    Eight modular poles deliver overlapping 360° scans. Each integrates range sensing, inertial measurement, and edge filtering before uplink to the CPU.

  • Data CPU

    Central fusion hub timestamps every return, harmonizes coordinate frames, and runs the real-time digital twin for row-by-row decision support.

  • Power Lines

    Radial low-voltage distribution feeds each pole with redundant runs to shield the network from agricultural equipment disturbances.

  • GPS Sync

    RTK-grade synchronization keeps clock drift below navigation tolerances, ensuring centimeter-accurate reconstructions across the array.

  • Coverage

    Outer radius extends to 16 m with a high-confidence 10 m inner band, enabling multi-row observation without sacrificing point density.

Key Research Insights

Distributed sensor fabric

Architectural modelling confirms that evenly spaced poles deliver consistent overlap, reducing blind spots from canopy occlusions and simplifying seasonal recalibration.

Time-aligned data fusion

GPS-disciplined clocks and deterministic network hops keep latency predictable, so fused point clouds maintain their integrity for tree-by-tree analytics.

Edge-to-core efficiency

Pre-filtering at the pole trims noise before uplink, allowing the central CPU to focus on mapping, classification, and actionable insights.

Operational resilience

The layout builds in redundant power and wireless fallbacks, enabling continuous harvest monitoring even when maintenance crews service individual poles.
