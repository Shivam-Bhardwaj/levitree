Central Laser & Sensor Pole Layout

A central green reference laser anchors ten perimeter poles spaced to cover a 100 m treatment block. Each pole carries a vertical array of LDRs behind optical filtering so the team can read precise beam height changes and infer how soil or slurry injection is shifting the ground plane in real time.

  • 532 nm Central Laser

    DPSS or diode module is current-driven and modulated at 5–20 kHz, generating a crisp green line that remains dominant after optical filtering.

  • Sensor Poles

    Ten poles host stacked LDRs spaced for 2 mm vertical resolution. Each assembly combines baffling, filters, and polarizers to isolate the reference beam against harsh daylight.

  • Signal Processing Hub

    Central controller synchronizes modulation, performs synchronous detection on pole feeds, and streams the reconstructed ground level to deployment dashboards.

  • Power & Data Backbone

    Ruggedized low-voltage runs or solar assists keep pole electronics powered, while shielded signal cables return intensity data to the hub.

  • Timing Reference

    Shared timing keeps modulation and lock-in amplifiers aligned, enabling precise subtraction of ambient frames and reduction of low-frequency noise.

  • Coverage Plan

    Ring spacing illustrates the targeted 50 m radius, giving the project a 100 m mapping span so simultaneous injection points can be verified.

Feasibility Highlights

Bench experiments with the green reference laser, stacked LDR poles, and optical filtering demonstrate that Levitree can monitor settlement during soil or slurry injection with millimeter precision while keeping the signal dominant in full sun.

Height resolution 2 mm

Spaced LDR ladders register where the laser line lands, giving per-pole ground displacement readings with 2 mm granularity after calibration.

Bandpass window 10 nm

Initial optical stack uses a 10 nm FWHM bandpass centered on 532 nm, with a 3–5 nm upgrade path when ambient spillover becomes critical.

Laser modulation 8–12 kHz

Central driver modulates within this band to dodge mains hum and pairs with lock-in demodulation so ambient frames can be subtracted per cycle.

Coverage grid 100 m

Ten poles distributed around the pit provide angular redundancy across a 100 m work zone, keeping injection-induced movement visible from every direction.

Ambient-Light Mitigation Models

Modelling runs quantify how spectral filtering, polarization, baffling, and temporal modulation suppress ambient noise so the green reference beam remains trackable in open-air daylight deployments.

Key Research Insights

Spectral filtering stack

Narrowband filters centered on the laser line with OD≥4 rejection outside the passband slash broadband skylight and LED interference before it hits the LDR ladder.

Polarization control

Aligning linear polarizers with the transmitter’s polarization axis buys double-digit dB of ambient rejection, with optional crossed filters to tame shiny equipment reflections.

Field-of-view discipline

Blackened baffles and tight apertures admit photons only from the laser stripe, reducing oblique glare while preserving enough signal for the lock-in stage.

Synchronous detection

Modulating the laser at kilohertz rates and sampling poles in ON/OFF pairs lets firmware subtract ambient frames in real time, stabilizing readings during cloud shifts.

Methodology & Validation Path

Progression followed a bench-to-field cadence: characterize optics indoors, stress the ambient rejection stack outside, assemble full poles, then rehearse alignment and timing with the central hub.

Phase 01

Optical bench characterization

Laser, filters, and LDR ladders were profiled to validate 2 mm resolution, filter passbands, and the need for OD≥4 rejection beyond 532 nm.

Phase 02

Ambient rejection trials

Outdoor tests in varying light levels tuned polarizer alignment, modulation rates, and lock-in filtering to guarantee >15 dB signal dominance at noon sun.

Phase 03

Pole integration build

Mechanical prototypes combined baffled housings, wiring harnesses, and quick-swap filters so each pole can be serviced without disturbing calibration.

Phase 04

Field alignment rehearsal

Ten-pole layout was staged to practice hub synchronization, ON/OFF sampling loops, and data ingest workflows ahead of a full remediation deployment.

Integration Considerations

Moving from feasibility to site deployment requires tight coordination across optics, firmware, and field operations so mitigation strategies stay effective throughout the remediation window.

Signal processing & firmware

  • Implement synchronized ON/OFF sampling and correlated double sampling in the pole firmware.
  • Expose per-pole gain and offset controls so technicians can retune after filter swaps.
  • Log modulation phase and SNR metrics to the hub for rapid diagnostics during injections.
  • Document the optical stack order — scene → bandpass → polarizer → aperture → sensor — to keep servicing consistent.

Field logistics & safety

  • Mount baffles and polarizers with keyed hardware to preserve alignment during transport.
  • Plan conduit routes that protect signal cabling from slurry hoses and heavy machinery.
  • Post Class 3R laser signage and provide remote interlocks for emergency beam shutdown.
