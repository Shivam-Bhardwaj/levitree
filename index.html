Central Laser & Sensor Pole Layout

Each pole carries a vertical array of LDRs behind optical filtering so the team can read precise beam height changes and infer how soil or slurry injection is shifting the ground plane in real time.

Ambient-Light Mitigation Models

Modelling runs quantify how spectral filtering, polarization, baffling, and temporal modulation suppress ambient noise so the green reference beam remains trackable in open-air daylight deployments.

Key Research Insights

Spectral filtering stack

Narrowband filters centered on the laser line with OD‚â•4 rejection outside the passband slash broadband skylight and LED interference before it hits the LDR ladder.

Polarization control

Aligning linear polarizers with the transmitter’s polarization axis buys double-digit dB of ambient rejection, with optional crossed filters to tame shiny equipment reflections.

Field-of-view discipline

Blackened baffles and tight apertures admit photons only from the laser stripe, reducing oblique glare while preserving enough signal for the lock-in stage.

Synchronous detection

Modulating the laser at kilohertz rates and sampling poles in ON/OFF pairs lets firmware subtract ambient frames in real time, stabilizing readings during cloud shifts.

Condition Ablation Report

A 48-hour ablation series varied pole spacing, illumination, and deliberate hardware disturbances to map out the operating envelope before committing to a remediation pilot. Results below isolate how each stressor affects lock-in margin and millimetre accuracy.

Distance envelope

Expanding pole spacing from 40 m to 90 m introduced additional beam divergence and atmospheric shimmer, costing roughly 3 dB of SNR per extra 20 m. Beyond 80 m the 2 mm resolution spec drifts to ¬±2.4 mm unless the aperture is tightened and poles are re-levelled.

Ambient illumination

Moving between direct sun, LED bay lighting, and twilight proved the layered filter stack: 5 nm bandpass + crossed polarizers recovered 18‚Äì22 dB of lock-in margin in noon sun, while twilight benefited from reopening the aperture and lengthening integration to preserve signal volume.

Coupled motion

An intentionally unpreferable movement of both emitter and receiver‚Äî0.35¬∞ hub yaw while a pole swayed ¬±9 mm at 0.8 Hz‚Äîintroduced a temporary +4‚Äì5 mm height bias. Guying the poles and triggering a rapid firmware re-zero pulled the drift back under ¬±1.8 mm within two sampling cycles.

Methodology & Validation Path

Progression followed a bench-to-field cadence: characterize optics indoors, stress the ambient rejection stack outside, assemble full poles, then rehearse alignment and timing with the central hub.

Phase 01

Optical bench characterization

Laser, filters, and LDR ladders were profiled to validate 2 mm resolution, filter passbands, and the need for OD‚â•4 rejection beyond 532 nm.

Phase 02

Ambient rejection trials

Outdoor tests in varying light levels tuned polarizer alignment, modulation rates, and lock-in filtering to guarantee >15 dB signal dominance at noon sun.

Phase 03

Pole integration build

Mechanical prototypes combined baffled housings, wiring harnesses, and quick-swap filters so each pole can be serviced without disturbing calibration.

Phase 04

Field alignment rehearsal

Ten-pole layout was staged to practice hub synchronization, ON/OFF sampling loops, and data ingest workflows ahead of a full remediation deployment.

Integration Considerations

Moving from feasibility to site deployment requires tight coordination across optics, firmware, and field operations so mitigation strategies stay effective throughout the remediation window.

Signal processing & firmware

  • Implement synchronized ON/OFF sampling and correlated double sampling in the pole firmware.
  • Expose per-pole gain and offset controls so technicians can retune after filter swaps.
  • Log modulation phase and SNR metrics to the hub for rapid diagnostics during injections.
  • Document the optical stack order ‚Äî scene ‚Üí bandpass ‚Üí polarizer ‚Üí aperture ‚Üí sensor ‚Äî to keep servicing consistent.

Field logistics & safety

  • Mount baffles and polarizers with keyed hardware to preserve alignment during transport.
  • Plan conduit routes that protect signal cabling from slurry hoses and heavy machinery.
  • Post Class 3R laser signage and provide remote interlocks for emergency beam shutdown.

Communication Protocols

  • RS-485 multi-drop bus: Half-duplex 921.6 kbps (8N1) with CRC-16 on each 64-byte frame; the hub polls four pole addresses in a 4 ms window to maintain deterministic latency.
  • Modulation sync line: LVDS pair carrying a 10 kHz square wave plus frame-start strobe so lock-in demodulators stay phase-aligned with the laser.
  • Uplink: Hub publishes JSON telemetry via MQTT over 100BASE-TX Ethernet (or Wi-Fi backhaul) every 250 ms with optional TLS and VPN tunneling for remote observers.

Power Budget

The minimal kit runs from a single 24 V DC source or field battery pack.

  • Laser head: 9.5 W typical, 14 W peak during TEC warm-up.
  • Sensor pole: 1.3 W @ 9 V for LDR bias, analog front end, and RS-485 node.
  • Central hub & GNSS: 4.1 W @ 5 V with Ethernet and MQTT active.

Total draw: ~17.6 W continuous for four poles; reserve 25 W to cover laser startup and environmental overhead.

Clock & Timing Sources

  • Hub MCU clock: 168 MHz core with 84 MHz timer driving dual 10 kHz PWM modulators.
  • ADC sampling: 200 kS/s aggregate per pole, performing four-phase synchronous demodulation into 24-bit accumulators.
  • 1PPS disciplining: GNSS 1PPS and 10 MHz VCXO hold modulation jitter below 80 ns and frequency error below ¬±2 ppm.
